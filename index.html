Biographical Summary

Navin Gupta, M.D. is an American physician-scientist and faculty member in the Division of Nephrology and Hypertension at Mayo Clinic in Rochester, Minnesota. Dr. Gupta specializes in the treatment of polycystic kidney disease (PKD) and other forms of genetic kidney disease, as well as acute kidney injury, chronic kidney disease, hypertension, kidney stones, and electrolyte abnormalities. He is certified in Internal Medicine and Nephrology by the American Board of Internal Medicine.

Prior to joining the faculty at Mayo Clinic in 2023, he spent 8 years at Harvard Medical School (HMS) and affiliated major teaching hospitals, ultimately serving as an Associate Physician at the Massachusetts General Hospital (MGH) and a faculty member at HMS. He is a graduate of the University of Massachusetts Medical School, of the internal medicine and chief medical residency at Saint Elizabeth's Medical Center (Boston, MA), and the Harvard combined Nephrology fellowship.

Dr. Gupta leads a basic science research program using kidney tissue derived from human stem cells, termed kidney organoids. Kidney organoids permit experimentation in human kidney tissue-in-a-dish, enabling translational research in disease modeling, drug discovery, and regenerative medicine. His research has garnered awards from the Brigham Research Institute of Brigham and Women's Hospital and the Harvard Stem Cell Research Institute (HSCI). His research has been supported by the National Institutes of Health and HSCI.

Kidney Organoids as a Window Into Disease

The Gupta Laboratory uses human stem cell–derived kidney organoids — miniature kidney-like structures grown from patient cells — to model and study kidney diseases at the molecular level, with a focus on PKD, DKD, AKI-to-CKD transition, amyloidosis, and gene therapy safety.

What is a Kidney Organoid?

A kidney organoid is a miniature, self-organizing kidney structure derived from human pluripotent stem cells. It contains nephron components — proximal tubules, glomeruli, distal tubules — arranged in three dimensions, enabling human-relevant disease modeling that animal models cannot replicate.

1
Patient-derived iPSCs reprogrammed from blood or skin cells
2
Directed differentiation into metanephric mesenchyme
3
Self-organization into 3D nephron-containing organoids
4
CRISPR editing, drug exposure, or disease gene introduction
5
Phenotyping: imaging, snRNA-seq, proteomics, functional assays
Disease Focus Areas
What We Study

Each research area is anchored by published findings from this laboratory, using kidney organoids as the primary experimental platform.

🔴

Polycystic Kidney Disease

PKD · ADPKD · ARPKD

The lab models cyst formation in kidney organoids derived from PKD patient cells and CRISPR-edited iPSCs. A key finding from the lab identified the PTH1R → cAMP → pPKA → pCREB axis as a driver of cystogenesis in developmental PKD — a novel signaling pathway with therapeutic implications. The lab also developed the first ARPKD organoid-on-a-chip model.

"Kidney organoids demonstrate that PTH1R drives a cystogenic cAMP-pPKA-pCREB axis in developmental polycystic kidney disease." — Afrin H, … Gupta N (PI). Am J Physiol 2025
Afrin et al. 2025 PKD Review 2025 ARPKD Chip 2022
🟠

Diabetic Kidney Disease

DKD · Tubular Atrophy · Fibrosis

Diabetic glomerulosclerosis (DGS) is the leading cause of end-stage kidney disease. The lab demonstrated that loss of FANCD2 in DNA-damaged proximal tubules correlates with fibrosis severity in human DGS biopsy samples — linking the organoid AKI-to-CKD model directly to the most common clinical form of progressive kidney disease.

"Kidney biopsy samples with DGS and moderate IFTA demonstrated loss of FANCD2 in DNA-damaged tubular cells, with serum creatinine inversely correlated to FANCD2 abundance." — Gupta N (1st author). Sci Transl Med 2022
Gupta et al. Sci Transl Med 2022
🟢

AKI → CKD Transition

Tubular Repair · FANCD2 · DNA Damage

A landmark study from this lab (Science Translational Medicine, 2022) used snRNA-seq of kidney organoids to identify homologous recombination (FANCD2/RAD51) as the molecular switch governing the transition from intrinsic repair to incomplete repair and CKD. The DNA ligase IV inhibitor SCR7 rescued FANCD2-mediated repair, preventing organoid atrophy — demonstrating drug discovery utility.

"HDR genes FANCD2, RAD51, BRCA2 were transiently upregulated during intrinsic repair but downregulated in incomplete repair. SCR7 rescued FANCD2 expression and limited tubular atrophy." — Gupta N†, Matsumoto T†, Hiratsuka K†. Sci Transl Med 2022
↓ PDF: Sci Transl Med 2022
🟣

AL Amyloidosis

Amyloid · Proteomic Analysis · Kidney Involvement

The lab contributed to a multi-center proteomics study investigating how AL amyloid plaques change after plasma cell-directed therapy. Using mass spectrometry-based proteomic profiling, this work characterized post-treatment shifts in amyloid composition, with implications for light chain-directed anti-fibril monoclonal antibody therapies and monitoring kidney amyloid response.

"Proteomic shifts in AL amyloid plaques post-plasma cell therapy identified compositional changes with potential implications for anti-fibril monoclonal antibodies." — Rees MJ, … Gupta N, … Kourelis T. Haematologica 2026
Rees et al. Haematologica 2026
🔵

Gene Therapy Nephrotoxicity

AAV · NF-κB · Safety

As gene therapy approaches move toward clinical application, understanding off-target kidney toxicity is critical. This lab demonstrated that AAV vectors used in gene therapy activate NF-κB inflammatory signaling in kidney organoids, producing a nephrotoxic response — establishing kidney organoids as a safety screening platform for gene therapy development.

"AAV for gene therapy drives a nephrotoxic response via NF-κB in kidney organoids." — Gupta N (1st author), Zhang K, Sabbisetti V, Shu J, Morizane R. Signal Transduct Target Ther 2025
Gupta et al. 2025
🩵

PTH Signaling & Tuberous Sclerosis

PTH1R · mTOR · Genetic Kidney Disease

The lab has active work on parathyroid hormone receptor 1 (PTH1R) signaling in kidney disease and cystogenesis, and has contributed to the clinical management of tuberous sclerosis complex (TSC) from a nephrologist's perspective — connecting mechanistic organoid work to the bedside management of genetic kidney diseases involving mTOR pathway dysregulation.

"Clinical Management of Tuberous Sclerosis: A Nephrologist's Perspective." — Robichaud JH, Afrin H, Torres VE, Gupta NR (PI). Kidney360 2025
Kidney360 2025 PTH/SIK Axis 2023
Experimental Platforms
How We Do It
🧫

3D Kidney Organoids

Human iPSC-derived organoids containing proximal tubules, glomeruli, distal tubules, and interstitium — grown from patient or CRISPR-edited stem cells.

💧

Organoid-on-a-Chip

Microfluidic platforms with perfusion flow that enhance organoid vascularization and maturation, enabling physiologically relevant disease modeling (Nat Methods 2019; Sci Adv 2022).

✂️

CRISPR Genome Editing

Targeted introduction of disease-causing mutations (e.g. PKD1, PKD2, TSC1/2) into iPSCs to generate isogenic disease models with precise genetic control.

🔬

Single-Nuclear RNA-seq

Whole-transcriptome profiling at single-cell resolution across basal, intrinsic repair, and incomplete repair states — used to identify MDM2, FANCD2, and RAD51 as key repair regulators (Sci Transl Med 2022).

💊

Drug Screening

Targeted compound screening using organoid injury models. The DNA ligase IV inhibitor SCR7 was identified as a therapeutic candidate for preventing AKI-to-CKD progression.

🧬

Human Biopsy Validation

Organoid findings are validated in human kidney biopsy cohorts — connecting molecular discoveries in-dish to clinical specimens from patients with DKD, AKI/ATN, and fibrosis.
